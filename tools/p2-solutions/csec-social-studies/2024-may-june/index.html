    Question 1 — Individual, Family and Society

    Monogamy; sibling household vs single-parent family; child-protection laws; community action against family poverty. (20 marks)

    (a) Define the term 'monogamy'. (2 marks)

    Monogamy is a form of marriage in which one person is legally married to only ONE partner at a time.

    Key idea for the 2 marks: (1) one spouse only, (2) at any given time. This is the system recognised by law in most Caribbean countries — it stands in contrast to polygamy (one person with several spouses).

    (b) Explain the difference between a sibling household and a single-parent family. (4 marks)

    Two clear differences, side-by-side:

    FeatureSibling householdSingle-parent family
    Who lives in the homeTwo or more brothers and/or sisters living together with NO parent or other adult guardian present.One parent (mother OR father) living with one or more of their own children.
    Who is the "head"The eldest sibling normally takes the role of head and caregiver — they cook, supervise schooling, and make decisions.The lone parent is clearly the head and the legal/financial guardian of the children.

    In short: a sibling household has children only (no adult parent); a single-parent family has one adult parent and their children.

    Marking: 2 marks for stating the composition of each, 2 marks for an explanation of the head/responsibility difference.

    (c) Outline TWO reasons why parents and other citizens should be aware of the laws relating to the care of children. (4 marks)
    1. Respect for human rights — protecting the rights and welfare of the child. Caribbean child-care laws (e.g., the Child Care & Protection Act, the Children Act) set out a child's right to food, shelter, schooling, medical care and freedom from abuse. Adults who know the law fulfil one of the syllabus' core civic duties — respect for human rights — by recognising neglect or abuse and reporting it to the relevant agency, ensuring children grow up safely.
    2. Being law-abiding — avoiding prosecution. Acts such as physical abuse, leaving young children unsupervised, removing a child from school, or child labour all carry fines or imprisonment. Awareness of the law allows parents to fulfil the civic duty of being law-abiding, keeping their parenting style legal and knowing their own rights and duties.

    2 marks per reason — 1 for the reason, 1 for the development.

    (d)(i) Suggest THREE actions that a community group may take to help families who are experiencing poverty. (6 marks)
    (d)(ii) Explain why EACH action suggested in (d)(i) is likely to be successful. (6 marks)

    Pair each action with its explanation. 2 marks per action (1 for the action, 1 for development) + 2 marks per explanation = 4 marks per row × 3 rows = 12 marks.

    ActionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Set up a community feeding programme / soup kitchen — volunteers cook hot meals daily and distribute food hampers to identified poor families. Hunger is the most immediate effect of poverty. A reliable hot meal frees the family's small income for rent, utilities and school costs, and keeps children healthy enough to attend school regularly.
    2. Run free skills-training and adult-education classes — sewing, cosmetology, plumbing, basic computing, food handling, CXC subjects. Skills give the unemployed parent a marketable trade so they can earn income or start a small business. This tackles the cause of the poverty (low employability), not just the symptom — a long-term solution.
    3. Lobby government and partner with NGOs / churches to bring services to the community — e.g., legal-aid clinics, applications for PATH/Public Assistance, free medical days. Many poor families don't claim the help they're entitled to because they don't know it exists or can't navigate the paperwork. A community group acts as a bridge, ensuring the family actually accesses housing grants, school-feeding programmes and welfare cheques.

    Other valid actions: micro-loan/savings club, school-uniform & book drive, mentorship for at-risk youth, community garden, Christmas treats. The mark scheme accepts any reasonable, locally-relevant suggestion as long as the explanation links it to poverty reduction.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 2 — Individual, Family and Society (Government)

    Group cohesion in a political party; bar-graph analysis of voter turnout; actions to raise voter turnout. (20 marks)

    (a)(i) Define the term 'group cohesion'. (2 marks)

    Group cohesion is the degree to which the members of a group are bonded together, share common goals, and work cooperatively to achieve them.

    Key marking points: (1) members are united / stick together, (2) common goals or shared values that hold them together.

    (a)(ii) State TWO characteristics of a group that might help to promote cohesion among members of a political party. (2 marks)
    1. A shared ideology / common political beliefs — when members all believe in the same vision for the country (e.g., free education, lower taxes), they are pulled together by that purpose.
    2. Strong, charismatic leadership — a respected leader who listens, communicates well and can resolve internal conflicts keeps members loyal and reduces breakaways.
    3. Other accepted: regular meetings & communication; clear constitution and rules; party symbols/colours/songs that build identity; rewards for loyalty (positions, recognition).

    1 mark each.

    (b) The bar graph shows voter turnout for Country X over five elections (2000–2020). Bars: 2000 ≈ 38 000, 2005 ≈ 35 000, 2010 ≈ 30 000, 2015 ≈ 22 000, 2020 ≈ 17 000.
    (b)(i) Describe the pattern of voting over the period 2000–2020. (2 marks)

    Voter turnout in Country X has shown a steady decline (a downward trend) over the twenty-year period — from about 38 000 voters in 2000 to about 17 000 voters in 2020, a fall of more than 50%.

    1 mark for identifying the downward trend, 1 mark for supporting it with figures from the graph.

    (b)(ii) Calculate the difference in the voter turnout between 2010 and 2020. (2 marks)

    Read the bars: 2010 turnout ≈ 30 000 voters; 2020 turnout ≈ 17 000 voters.

    Difference = 30 000 − 17 000 = 13 000 voters

    1 mark for setting up the subtraction with the correct two values, 1 mark for the answer. (If your reading of the bars differs slightly, the examiner accepts any answer within ±2 000 of the correct figure.)

    (c)(i) Suggest THREE actions that a political party may take to help increase voter turnout. (6 marks)
    (c)(ii) Explain why EACH action is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    ActionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Run a voter-education / civic-engagement campaign — door-to-door canvassing, town halls, school visits, social-media explainers on how to register and vote. Many citizens — especially first-time voters — stay home because they don't know how the process works, where their polling station is, or what ID they need. Clear information removes that barrier and converts non-voters into voters.
    2. Provide free transportation to polling stations on election day — buses or shared taxis from each community. In rural Caribbean districts and among elderly or low-income voters, the cost and difficulty of getting to the polling station is a real barrier. Free, organised transport makes voting effortless and lifts turnout significantly.
    3. Use social media and influencer endorsements to target young voters (18–35) with messages tied to issues they care about — jobs, housing, climate. Young people are the largest non-voting group in the Caribbean. Reaching them on the platforms they actually use, with messages they relate to, makes politics feel relevant and increases the chance they will turn out.

    Other valid actions: hold rallies and political meetings to energise the base; field youthful candidates; manifesto launches with practical, costed promises; partner with churches and community groups to mobilise members.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 3 — Sustainable Development and Use of Resources

    Sources of water pollution; renewable vs non-renewable; how nutrition/health and education develop human resources; CARICOM actions to reduce global warming. (20 marks)

    (a) State TWO sources of water pollution. (2 marks)
    1. Industrial waste — factories discharging chemicals, oil and heavy metals into rivers and the sea (e.g., bauxite red-mud spills, sugar-factory effluent).
    2. Sewage and domestic waste — untreated household sewage entering waterways from leaking septic tanks or direct outfalls.
    3. Other accepted: agricultural run-off (fertilisers, pesticides, animal waste); oil spills from ships; improper garbage disposal; mining run-off.

    1 mark each.

    (b) Outline the difference between a renewable resource and a non-renewable resource. (2 marks)

    Renewable resource: a natural resource that can replace itself within a human lifetime, either continuously (sunlight, wind) or by re-growth (forests, fish stocks). It will not run out provided the rate of use is below the rate of renewal.

    Non-renewable resource: a resource that exists in a fixed quantity on Earth and is used up faster than it can be re-formed (fossil fuels — oil, natural gas, coal — and most minerals like bauxite). Once used, it is effectively gone forever.

    1 mark per definition. The contrast — replenishable vs finite/exhaustible — is what is being tested.

    (c) Explain how EACH of the following factors contributes to the development of human resources.
    (c)(i) Nutrition and health. (2 marks)

    "Human resources" means the people of a country and the productive skills they carry.

    A well-fed, healthy workforce turns up to work, concentrates better and is more productive. Children who eat balanced meals develop their brains properly and perform better in school, producing the next generation of skilled workers. Lower rates of illness mean less time lost to sickness and lower healthcare costs for the state — money that can be re-invested in development.

    1 mark for linking good nutrition/health to productivity / cognitive development; 1 mark for a developed point.

    (c)(ii) Education. (2 marks)

    Education equips citizens with the literacy, numeracy and technical skills a modern economy demands. An educated workforce can run hospitals, schools, banks, IT firms and tourism businesses; it attracts foreign investment, raises wages, reduces poverty and fuels innovation. Education also creates better-informed citizens who participate more fully in society and government.

    1 mark for skills/training point; 1 mark for development (productivity, innovation, attracting investment, social mobility).

    (d)(i) Suggest THREE actions that residents of CARICOM countries may take to help reduce global warming. (6 marks)
    (d)(ii) Explain why EACH action is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    ActionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Switch to renewable energy at home — install solar panels and solar water heaters, swap incandescent bulbs for LED. Electricity in most CARICOM islands is generated by burning imported diesel and bunker oil, which release large amounts of CO₂. Every household that goes solar removes that demand from the grid, directly cutting greenhouse-gas emissions and the country's fuel-import bill.
    2. Reduce private-vehicle use — carpool, take public transport, walk or cycle for short trips, and choose hybrid or electric vehicles when buying. Vehicle exhaust is one of the largest single sources of CO₂ in Caribbean cities. Fewer cars on the road = less petrol burned = less CO₂. Carpooling and EVs also reduce traffic congestion and improve air quality, which has secondary health benefits.
    3. Plant trees and protect forests — community tree-planting drives, replant after hurricanes, refuse to clear hillsides for housing. Trees absorb CO₂ from the atmosphere through photosynthesis and store it as wood — they are nature's "carbon sinks". A forested island captures far more carbon than a deforested one, and the forest also reduces flooding, landslides and soil erosion.

    Other valid actions: reduce-reuse-recycle to cut landfill methane; eat less imported red meat; insulate homes / use natural ventilation instead of A/C; support eco-friendly local businesses; pressure government to sign and enforce climate treaties.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 4 — Regional Integration (Essay)

    "The Development of the CARICOM Single Market and Economy (CSME)" — five-bullet structured essay. (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Define the term 'regional integration'. (2 marks)

    Regional integration is the process by which neighbouring countries within a geographical region come together and agree to cooperate on political, economic, social and cultural matters in order to achieve common goals — such as free movement of goods, people and capital, and a stronger collective voice in world affairs.

    1 mark for the idea of countries joining/cooperating; 1 mark for a purpose (economic / political / collective benefit). CARICOM is the obvious Caribbean example.

    Bullet 2: Outline TWO objectives of the CSME. (4 marks)
    1. Free movement of goods, services, capital and labour — to remove tariffs and work-permit barriers between member states so that a Jamaican welder can take up a job in Trinidad, or a Bajan business can sell rum to Guyana, without crossing trade barriers. This creates a single, larger market of more than 18 million people.
    2. The right of CARICOM nationals to establish a business in any member state on the same terms as locals (the "right of establishment"), and a common external tariff (CET) so the region presents a united front in trade negotiations with the rest of the world.
    3. Other accepted: harmonisation of laws and standards; coordinated foreign-trade policy; a common currency in the long term.

    2 marks per objective — 1 for naming, 1 for outlining. Another widely-cited CSME objective is the CARICOM Development Fund (established 2008), which provides financial and technical assistance to disadvantaged member states so that smaller economies benefit equally from the single market.

    Bullet 3: Explain how either music OR sport helps to promote regional integration among CARICOM countries. (3 marks)

    Sport — cricket.

    The West Indies cricket team represents all CARICOM nations playing as one — Jamaicans, Bajans, Trinis, Guyanese, Antiguans, Vincentians and others wear the same maroon cap. When the team wins (e.g., the T20 World Cup victories in 2012 and 2016), the entire region celebrates as one people. This builds a shared Caribbean identity that transcends national borders (1 mark).

    Regional tournaments such as the CARIFTA Games and the CPL (Caribbean Premier League) bring athletes, coaches, fans and tourist spending across borders, generating economic activity and personal friendships between citizens of different islands (1 mark).

    Sports cooperation also produces shared institutions — Cricket West Indies, the Caribbean Football Union — which set common standards and require regional decision-making, deepening institutional integration (1 mark).

    (Music alternative: reggae, soca, calypso, chutney and dancehall cross island boundaries; festivals like Reggae Sumfest, Trinidad Carnival and Crop Over draw Caribbean visitors and showcase a shared culture, building a sense of "one Caribbean people".)

    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE actions that governments may take to promote regional integration. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain why EACH action is likely to be successful. (6 marks → 5 marks in some mark schemes)
    Government actionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Remove all remaining travel barriers — implement a single CARICOM passport / e-gate, abolish work-permits for skilled CARICOM nationals, harmonise immigration forms. When citizens can move, work and study freely across the region, real human links form — labour fills shortages where they exist (e.g., teachers, nurses), money flows in remittances, and a Caribbean identity is reinforced. Free movement is the lived reality of integration.
    2. Strengthen regional transport links — invest jointly in LIAT/inter-island shipping, fund a regional airline, build a CARICOM ferry network and reliable cargo routes. Integration cannot work if it is cheaper to fly Kingston–Miami than Kingston–Bridgetown. Affordable, reliable transport unlocks intra-regional trade and tourism, lowers prices for consumers, and physically connects the region.
    3. Harmonise education and qualifications — push CXC/CSEC and CAPE region-wide, mutual recognition of degrees and trade certificates, joint scholarships at UWI. Common qualifications mean a Belizean nurse's licence is automatically valid in St Vincent. This makes free movement of labour practical, raises overall skill levels, and builds shared regional institutions that students grow up using.

    Other valid actions: ratify and honour CARICOM protocols — abide by the Treaty of Chaguaramas and its revisions, adopt the Caribbean Court of Justice as the final court of appeal, and respect agreed CSME rules on trade and free movement; launch a CARICOM-wide currency / payment system; coordinate foreign policy at the UN; sign joint trade agreements (CARIFORUM-EU); fund a regional disaster-response agency (CDEMA) more generously; coordinate marketing of "Brand Caribbean" tourism.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 5 — Section B Option: Communication

    "Communication and Culture in the Caribbean" — five-bullet essay. (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Define the term 'communication'. (2 marks)

    Communication is the process by which information, ideas, feelings or instructions are transferred from a sender to a receiver, through a chosen channel (spoken word, writing, gesture, image or signal), with the aim of producing a shared understanding.

    1 mark for the transfer-of-information idea; 1 mark for the sender → channel → receiver structure.

    Bullet 2: Describe ONE form of communication used in the Caribbean. (2 marks)

    Mass media — radio. Radio is a form of electronic, one-to-many communication. A presenter speaks into a microphone in a studio; the signal is broadcast over FM or AM frequencies and is picked up simultaneously by thousands of listeners on cars, phones and home radios across the country. It carries news, music, talk shows, public-service announcements and advertising — and remains the most accessible mass medium in rural Caribbean areas because it works without electricity (battery sets) and without literacy.

    1 mark for naming/describing the form; 1 mark for an accurate detail (range, audience, technology).

    Bullet 3: Explain TWO ways in which language can affect communication in the Caribbean region. (4 marks)
    1. Multiple official languages create barriers. CARICOM contains English-speaking (Jamaica, Trinidad), Dutch-speaking (Suriname), French-speaking (Haiti) and Spanish-speaking neighbours. Without translation, business meetings, regional conferences and trade documents can be misunderstood, slowing down integration. (2 marks)
    2. Creole / dialect vs Standard English. Most Caribbean people speak a creole at home (Jamaican Patois, Trinidadian Creole, Bajan dialect) but Standard English is the language of school, courts and formal employment. A speaker who is fluent in patois but weak in Standard English may struggle to write a job application, give evidence in court, or understand a legal contract — leading to miscommunication and disadvantage. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: language as a marker of identity (binds in-groups, excludes outsiders); rapid spread of slang via social media changes meaning across generations; non-verbal cues (kissing teeth, hand gestures) carry meaning lost on non-Caribbean people.
    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE strategies that a government agency responsible for culture might use to preserve and promote culture. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain why EACH strategy is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    StrategyWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Mandate cultural studies in school curricula — folk songs, local history, indigenous languages, traditional crafts taught from primary level. Culture is preserved by being passed on. Catching children early and embedding it in compulsory schooling guarantees every new generation knows their heritage, even if their families do not actively practise it.
    2. Fund and televise national festivals — Carnival, Jonkonnu, Hosay, Emancipation Day; provide grants for masquerade bands and steel-pan orchestras. State funding keeps these expensive cultural productions alive when commercial sponsorship fluctuates. Broadcasting them brings the festivals into every home and creates pride; tourists and the diaspora watch too, reinforcing the country's cultural "brand".
    3. Set up a National Cultural Heritage Register and museums; digitise oral histories, photographs and artefacts; protect listed historic buildings by law. A formal register creates a permanent record that survives the death of elders and the rebuilding of old districts. Once items are legally protected, they cannot be quietly lost or sold, and researchers and tourists have a place to access them.

    Other valid strategies: scholarships in cultural arts; partnerships with regional bodies (CARIFESTA); social-media campaigns aimed at youth; protect intangible heritage (recipes, language) through UNESCO listing.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 6 — Section B Option: Consumer Affairs

    "Improving Consumer Protection" — five-bullet essay. (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Define the term 'consumer'. (2 marks)

    A consumer is any person, household or organisation that buys and uses goods and services to satisfy their needs and wants — they are the final user, not someone reselling for profit.

    Bullet 2: State TWO factors that may lead to an increase in consumers' demand for a good or service. (2 marks)
    1. A rise in income — when wages, salaries or remittances rise, consumers can afford more, and their demand for normal goods (cars, restaurant meals, smartphones) increases.
    2. Effective advertising / change in taste — a clever ad campaign or a new fashion trend (e.g., a new sneaker drop) sparks fresh demand for that item.
    3. Other accepted: a fall in price; a rise in the price of a substitute good; an increase in population; favourable changes in season/weather; expectation of future shortage.

    1 mark each.

    Bullet 3: Explain TWO ways in which consumers can be exploited. (4 marks)
    1. Sale of substandard or expired goods. Some traders re-package expired tinned food, sell counterfeit medicines, or stock electronics that fail the day after purchase. The consumer pays full price and receives a product that is unsafe or non-functional. (2 marks)
    2. Misleading advertising and hidden charges. A bank may advertise a "0% loan" only for the consumer to discover heavy processing fees and insurance costs in the fine print; a phone company may advertise "unlimited data" that is throttled after 5 GB. The buyer is induced into a contract on false impressions. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: short-weighing / short-measuring; price gouging during shortages; refusal to give refunds on faulty goods; misleading "BOGO" deals; pyramid schemes; loan-sharking with unlawful interest rates.
    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE actions that consumers may take to protect themselves from being exploited. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain why EACH action is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    ActionWhy it works
    1. Check labels, expiry dates, weights and read all contracts BEFORE paying. Most exploitation can be spotted at the moment of purchase. A 30-second inspection of an expiry date or a careful reading of a loan's fine print prevents the loss before it happens — prevention is cheaper than chasing a refund later.
    2. Always demand and keep a receipt; pay by card or transfer where possible. A receipt is legal proof of the transaction. Without one, no consumer-affairs commission, court or warranty desk can help. Card/digital payments leave an additional electronic trail and offer chargeback protection.
    3. Report exploitation to the Consumer Affairs Commission, BSJ/BSO or Bureau of Standards. Regulators have the legal power to fine traders, withdraw licences and order refunds. Consistent reporting also creates a public record that warns other consumers (e.g., via the Commission's website) — exploitation thrives in silence and dies in the open.

    Other valid actions: join a consumer-protection association; comparison-shop; stay informed via local news/social media; refuse aggressive door-to-door selling; learn one's rights under the Consumer Protection Act.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 7 — Section B Option: Tourism

    "The Importance of Tourism to the Caribbean Region" — five-bullet essay. (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Define the term 'economic linkage'. (2 marks)

    An economic linkage is a productive connection between one sector of the economy and another, in which the output of one industry is used as an input by another — so that the growth of one sector causes growth in the other.

    Example: a hotel (tourism) buying tomatoes from a local farmer (agriculture) is a backward linkage; a fish-export company supplying a restaurant chain is a forward linkage.

    Bullet 2: Explain TWO ways in which Caribbean countries benefit economically from the tourist industry. (4 marks)
    1. Foreign-exchange earnings. Tourists pay in US dollars, Pounds, Euros and Canadian dollars when they book hotels, dine, shop and pay departure taxes. This hard currency funds the imports of fuel, food and machinery that small Caribbean economies depend on, and stabilises the local exchange rate. (2 marks)
    2. Employment and income. Tourism is labour-intensive — every hotel, attraction, taxi-cooperative and restaurant employs cooks, drivers, housekeepers, tour guides, security and entertainers. In countries like Antigua, Barbados and the Bahamas, more than one in three jobs is tourism-linked. Wages and tips circulate through the local economy via the multiplier effect. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: government tax revenue (room tax, departure tax, VAT on services); investment in infrastructure (airports, roads, port upgrades); diversification away from a single export crop.
    Bullet 3: Outline ONE way in which Caribbean governments may support the development of the tourist industry. (2 marks)

    Tax incentives and concessions for tourism investors — granting tax holidays, duty-free importation of building materials and equipment, and reduced corporate-tax rates to companies that build new hotels, marinas or attractions. These incentives lower the cost of investment, attract foreign and local investors, and lead to new resort capacity, jobs and government earnings down the line.

    1 mark for the action, 1 mark for the development.

    Other accepted: destination-marketing campaigns through the Caribbean Tourism Organization; upgrading airports and cruise-ship piers; training programmes through hospitality institutes; visa relaxations for major source markets; safety/policing in tourist zones.

    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE ways in which the business sector may increase the linkages between tourism and other sectors of the economy. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain why EACH way is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    Action by business sectorWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Hotels source fresh produce, meat and fish from local farmers and fishermen — sign supply contracts with farmers' cooperatives. This converts tourist spending into income for farmers, expands the agricultural sector, and reduces foreign-exchange leakage from food imports. Tourists also prefer authentic local cuisine, so the hotels gain a market advantage.
    2. Tour operators package excursions through local craft villages, music studios and cultural sites — calypso shows, plantation tours, pottery workshops. Spreads tourist money beyond the all-inclusive bubble, creating income for artists and craftspeople. Authentic cultural experiences also raise visitor satisfaction and repeat visits.
    3. Hotels train and hire from local hospitality schools and source uniforms, soaps and furniture from local manufacturers. Builds the local services and manufacturing sectors and keeps wages circulating in the country. Trained local staff also reduce the need for expatriate hires and cut down on profits being repatriated overseas.
