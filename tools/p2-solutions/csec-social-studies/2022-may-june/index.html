    Question 1 — Individual, Family and Society

    Cultural diversity in the Caribbean; changing roles of family members; strategies to improve care of the elderly. (20 marks)

    (a)(i) Define the term 'cultural diversity'. (2 marks)

    Cultural diversity is the existence of a wide variety of distinct ethnic, racial, religious, linguistic and cultural groups living together within the same society — each with its own customs, beliefs, foods, music, dress and traditions, all contributing to the overall identity of the society.

    1 mark for "variety of cultures within a society"; 1 mark for naming dimensions of difference (race, religion, language, customs, food, etc.).

    (a)(ii) State TWO factors that contribute to cultural diversity in the Caribbean. (2 marks)
    1. The transatlantic slave trade and African forced migration — millions of West Africans were brought to the Caribbean, contributing African languages, religions, foods and music.
    2. Indian and Chinese indentureship after Emancipation (1838) — adding Hindu and Muslim religions, curry, roti, Diwali and Eid traditions, especially in Trinidad and Guyana.
    3. Other accepted: European colonisation by different powers (British, French, Spanish, Dutch); the survival of indigenous Amerindian peoples; Syrian-Lebanese and Portuguese trader migrations; modern globalisation and tourism.

    1 mark each.

    (b) The roles of family members in the Caribbean have been changing. Explain ONE cause AND ONE effect of the changes in the roles of family members. (4 marks)

    Cause — increased participation of women in the workforce. More Caribbean women now hold full-time paid jobs and tertiary qualifications than ever before. With both parents (or a single mother) working, the traditional model of the father as sole breadwinner and the mother as full-time homemaker has broken down. Children are also expected to take on chores earlier, and grandparents and other relatives often step in as daytime caregivers. (2 marks)

    Effect — sharing of domestic and parenting responsibilities. Fathers are now far more involved in cooking, school runs, ironing uniforms and helping with homework — tasks once considered "women's work". This produces a more equal partnership in modern Caribbean households, but it can also create role-conflict and tension where older relatives still expect the traditional pattern. Children too grow up with a different mental model of gender roles than their parents had. (2 marks)

    Other accepted causes: migration of one parent abroad; rise in single-parent households; the influence of media; legal changes giving women equal rights; technology making domestic work easier.

    Other accepted effects: rise in barrel children / skipped-generation households; greater financial independence for women; later age at marriage and childbearing; weaker authority of the father-figure.

    (c)(i) Suggest THREE strategies that the agency can recommend to improve the care of the elderly. (6 marks)
    (c)(ii) Explain how EACH strategy suggested in (c)(i) is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    StrategyWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Establish day-care centres and community visitation programmes for senior citizens, with trained social workers visiting shut-ins. Many elderly Caribbean people live alone after children migrate. Day centres provide meals, medical checks, exercise and social interaction in one place; home visits catch hidden cases of neglect, malnutrition or untreated illness early.
    2. Run training and licensing programmes for caregivers and family members — first-aid, dementia care, safe lifting techniques, recognising elder abuse. Most ill-treatment of the elderly comes from untrained relatives who become frustrated and overwhelmed. Trained caregivers handle bedridden, diabetic or dementia-affected seniors with patience and proper technique, dramatically improving quality of life.
    3. Pass and enforce stronger legislation — an Elder Abuse Act, mandatory reporting by health workers, registration and inspection of nursing homes, increased pension and NIS benefits. Laws give victims, social workers and police the legal tools to act. Inspections weed out unsafe nursing homes. Better pensions reduce the dependency of the elderly on relatives and let them afford basic dignity (food, medicine, transport) on their own.

    Other valid strategies: public awareness/sensitisation campaigns ("respect the elders"); subsidised medications and home modifications; tax incentives for families caring for relatives; intergenerational programmes pairing schoolchildren with seniors.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 2 — Institutions, Cohesion and Voter Behaviour

    Group cohesion definition + factor; functions of institutions; low voter turnout among 18–35-year-olds. (20 marks)

    (a)(i) Define the term 'group cohesion'. (2 marks)

    Group cohesion is the degree to which members of a group are united, bonded together by common goals, and committed to working cooperatively for the success of the group.

    1 mark for "members are united / bound together"; 1 mark for "common goals / cooperation."

    (a)(ii) Outline ONE factor which can contribute to improving group cohesion in an institution. (2 marks)

    Strong, fair leadership. A leader who communicates clearly, listens to members, treats everyone equitably and resolves conflict promptly earns trust. When members trust the leader, they buy into the institution's vision and feel safe expressing themselves — this binds the group together and reduces factionalism.

    1 mark for naming the factor; 1 mark for outlining how it operates.

    Other accepted factors: shared goals/mission, regular communication, team-building activities, fair rewards, clear rules and roles, pride in identity.

    (b) Describe TWO functions which an institution can serve within a society. (4 marks)
    1. Socialisation. Institutions like the family, schools, churches and the media teach individuals the values, norms, language and skills they need to function in society. The school socialises a child into being punctual, respecting authority, and reading; the church teaches morality. Without this function, every generation would have to invent culture from scratch. (2 marks)
    2. Provision of essential services and order. Economic institutions (banks, factories) provide goods and jobs; political institutions (Parliament, the courts) maintain law and order; health institutions (hospitals, clinics) provide medical care. Society is too complex for individuals to provide these for themselves — institutions specialise and serve everyone. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted functions: social control (police, courts enforcing laws); cultural transmission (museums, festivals); reproduction and care of the young (family); welfare and redistribution (NIS, charities); economic production and distribution.
    (c) Low voter turnout among voters aged 18–35.
    (c)(i) Suggest THREE factors that may explain low voter turnout among this age group. (6 marks)
    (c)(ii) Outline ONE action that may be taken by a political party to address EACH of the factors. (6 marks)
    Factor (why young people don't vote)Action a political party can take
    1. Apathy & cynicism — many young people feel that "all politicians are corrupt" and that their single vote will not change anything. They have grown up watching scandals and unfulfilled manifestos. Field young, fresh-faced candidates with clean records and publish a costed, time-bound, easy-to-track manifesto. Visible accountability dashboards (online) restore the sense that the vote is meaningful and the party can be trusted.
    2. Issues do not seem relevant — campaigns talk about pensions, road repair and 1970s-era political feuds rather than housing affordability, student loans, jobs in tech, mental health and climate change. Build a "Youth Manifesto" with concrete policies on jobs, affordable housing, student-loan relief and digital-economy training. Promote it through TikTok, Instagram Reels and YouTube using influencers and youth ambassadors.
    3. Practical access barriers — confusion about how to register, where to vote, ID requirements, polling stations far from college campuses or workplaces. Run on-campus and on-workplace registration drives, partner with the EOJ/EBC for "First-Time Voter" guides, and offer free transport to polling stations on election day.

    Other valid factors: distrust of the media; influence of social media misinformation; lack of civic education in school; migration of young people abroad. Other valid actions: open student-wing branches, host policy hackathons, sponsor sporting/cultural events, simplify online registration.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 3 — Sustainable Development and Use of Resources

    Renewable vs non-renewable; map of CARICOM (lumbering & bauxite); geothermal energy; CARICOM actions to reduce global warming. (20 marks)

    (a) State the difference between renewable and non-renewable resources. (2 marks)

    Renewable resources are natural resources that can regenerate or replenish themselves within a human time-scale — either continuously (sunlight, wind, tides) or by re-growth (forests, fish stocks, freshwater). Properly managed, they will not run out.

    Non-renewable resources exist in a fixed quantity on Earth and are used up far faster than they can be re-formed. Once burned or extracted they are essentially gone forever. Examples include fossil fuels (oil, coal, natural gas) and most metallic minerals (bauxite, gold).

    1 mark for each correctly contrasted definition.

    (b)(i) Place the letter X in a CARICOM country in South America where lumbering is important. (1 mark)

    The two CARICOM mainland countries in South America are Guyana and Suriname. Both have major lumbering industries — placing your X in either is correct. (Most students place X on Guyana, the larger English-speaking CARICOM member known for its hardwoods such as greenheart, purpleheart and mora.)

    (b)(ii) Place the letter Y in a CARICOM country where bauxite production is important. (1 mark)

    Place Y on Jamaica (the most famous Caribbean bauxite producer) OR on Guyana or Suriname (also major bauxite producers). Any of these three is correct.

    (c)(i) Define the term 'geothermal energy'. (1 mark)

    Geothermal energy is heat energy generated and stored within the Earth's crust — produced by the natural decay of radioactive elements and residual heat from the planet's formation — which can be tapped (typically as hot water or steam from underground) to generate electricity or heat buildings.

    (c)(ii) Identify ONE CARICOM member state in the Eastern Caribbean that has good potential for geothermal energy. (1 mark)

    Dominica (the leading example — currently developing a geothermal power station).

    Other accepted: Saint Lucia, St Vincent & the Grenadines, St Kitts & Nevis, Montserrat — all volcanic islands of the Lesser Antilles arc with significant geothermal potential.

    (c)(iii) Explain the relationship between economic development and the use of natural resources. (3 marks)

    Economic development depends heavily on natural resources because most early-stage growth is built on extracting and processing them. Trinidad's wealth comes from oil and natural gas; Jamaica's from bauxite and tourism (which itself relies on the natural resource of climate, beaches and reefs); Guyana's recent boom is from offshore oil. Resources provide raw materials for industry, energy for production, exports for foreign exchange and jobs for citizens (1 mark).

    However, the relationship cuts both ways. Heavy reliance on a single resource produces vulnerable, mono-economic nations (the "resource curse" — see Trinidad's gas-price crashes). Over-extraction destroys the very environment that supports tourism, agriculture and fisheries — leading to long-term decline (1 mark).

    Sustainable economic development therefore requires that resource use be managed: renewables substituted for non-renewables, profits reinvested in education and infrastructure, and environmental safeguards put in place so future generations can also develop (1 mark).

    (d)(i) Suggest THREE actions governments in the Caribbean may take to reduce the region's contribution to global warming. (6 marks)
    (d)(ii) Explain how EACH action suggested in (d)(i) is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    Government actionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Invest in large-scale renewable energy — solar farms, wind farms, geothermal plants (Dominica), hydroelectric projects. Most Caribbean electricity comes from imported diesel and bunker oil — heavy CO₂ emitters. Replacing them with renewables directly reduces national emissions and cuts the foreign-exchange drain on fuel imports. Once built, renewables are essentially fuel-free.
    2. Pass and enforce strict environmental laws — emissions limits on factories, vehicle inspections, mandatory environmental impact assessments, hefty fines for illegal logging and burning. Voluntary compliance does not work where short-term profit is at stake. Legal teeth make polluting expensive and force industry to adopt cleaner processes. Reforestation laws ensure carbon sinks are restored, and EIAs prevent damaging projects from being approved.
    3. Subsidise public transport and electric vehicles — modern buses, BRT lanes, rail where feasible, tax holidays on imported EVs and charging stations. Transport is one of the largest CO₂ sources in Caribbean cities. Affordable public transport pulls cars off the road; EV incentives flip new-vehicle purchases away from petrol/diesel. Both reduce emissions and improve urban air quality.

    Other valid actions: climate-smart agriculture; mangrove and coral-reef restoration; energy-efficiency standards for new buildings; banning single-use plastics; signing & honouring international climate treaties (Paris Agreement); regional cooperation through CCCCC (the Caribbean Community Climate Change Centre).

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 4 — Regional Integration (Essay)

    "Regional Integration: Challenges and Possibilities" — five-bullet structured essay. (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: State the difference between a bilateral and a multilateral agreement. (2 marks)

    Bilateral agreement: a formal agreement between TWO countries (e.g., the Jamaica–USA tax treaty; a trade deal between Trinidad and Guyana).

    Multilateral agreement: a formal agreement among THREE OR MORE countries acting together — e.g., the Treaty of Chaguaramas establishing CARICOM, the Paris Climate Agreement, the WTO.

    1 mark for each correctly contrasted definition.

    Bullet 2: Identify TWO Caribbean organisations that attempted to promote regional integration before the formation of CARICOM. (2 marks)
    1. The West Indies Federation (1958–1962) — the political union of ten British Caribbean colonies that aimed at full independence as a single nation.
    2. CARIFTA — the Caribbean Free Trade Association (1968–1973) — a free-trade area set up after the Federation collapsed, the direct forerunner of CARICOM.
    3. Other accepted: the Regional Shipping Council; the West Indies Sugar Association; the British West Indies political bodies of the 1940s; the University College of the West Indies (1948).

    1 mark each.

    Bullet 3: Outline TWO factors that contributed to the failure of the regional political union (the West Indies Federation) in 1962. (4 marks)
    1. Insularity and competing national interests. Each colony saw itself first and the Federation second. Jamaica — the largest member with the most to contribute and the most to lose — held a referendum in 1961 in which 54% voted to leave. Without Jamaica, the rest could not survive: as Eric Williams of Trinidad famously said, "one from ten leaves nought." (2 marks)
    2. Differences in size, wealth and stage of development. Jamaica and Trinidad were far richer and more populous than the smaller islands; they feared having to subsidise everyone else and being out-voted by the smaller members. Disputes over freedom of movement, taxation powers and the location of the federal capital (Chaguaramas) widened the rift. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted factors: weak federal government with very limited powers; geographic separation by sea making travel and communication costly; lack of a strong, unifying federal political party; colonial powers gave the Federation little real authority.
    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE actions that Caribbean governments may take to increase the movement of capital, goods and labour within the region. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain how EACH action is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    Government actionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Fully implement the CSME's free movement of skilled labour — automatic mutual recognition of qualifications, abolish work-permit requirements for CARICOM Skilled Nationals, harmonise immigration procedures, issue a common CARICOM passport. The biggest brake on intra-regional labour movement is administrative. Removing it allows nurses, teachers, ICT professionals and tradespeople to fill shortages where they exist, raising productivity in destination states and reducing unemployment in source states.
    2. Strengthen regional shipping, air and digital infrastructure — invest jointly in LIAT/inter-island shipping lines, fund a regional airline, build a CARICOM ferry network, deploy region-wide submarine cables. Goods cannot move freely without affordable freight; capital cannot move without reliable digital banking. Improved transport reduces shipping costs by 20–30%, making intra-regional trade competitive with extra-regional imports for the first time.
    3. Harmonise financial regulation and create a regional payment system — common stock-exchange listings (CSE), shared standards for banking and insurance, a real-time CARICOM payments platform. Capital flows where it can move quickly and safely. A harmonised regulatory environment lets Bajan investors put money into a Trinidadian start-up as easily as into a local one, deepening regional capital markets and attracting outside investment to the region as a whole.

    Other valid actions: enforce the Common External Tariff (CET); remove non-tariff barriers (quotas, paperwork); fund the CARICOM Development Fund to support less-developed members; sign and use the Caribbean Court of Justice as the final court of appeal for dispute resolution.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 5 — Section B Option: Communication

    "Preserving and Promoting the Culture of the Caribbean Region". (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Describe TWO forms of communication used in the Caribbean. (4 marks)
    1. Mass media — television. An electronic, one-to-many form of communication. Programmes are produced in studios and broadcast (over the air, by cable, or by satellite) so that thousands of viewers can simultaneously watch news, dramas, sports and adverts. TV combines moving images with sound, making it powerful for cultural transmission (carnival broadcasts, regional cricket, telenovelas). (2 marks)
    2. Social media — interactive online platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, TikTok and WhatsApp. This is many-to-many and two-way: any user can both publish content and respond to it. Caribbean people use it to share music, dance challenges, news, opinions and family videos with friends and the diaspora overseas, in real time. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: radio; newspapers; gestures & body language; public meetings/town halls; the postal service; oral storytelling; e-mail; podcasts.
    Bullet 2: Explain TWO ways in which communications technology supports regional integration. (4 marks)
    1. Faster, cheaper inter-island communication and decision-making. Video-conferencing (Zoom, Teams) lets the CARICOM Secretariat hold ministerial meetings with members in 15 different countries without the cost and time of flying everyone to Georgetown. Decisions that once took months now take days. Officials, students at UWI's distributed campuses, and businesses across the region can collaborate in real time. (2 marks)
    2. Cultural exchange and shared identity. Streaming platforms, YouTube and social media let Caribbean citizens consume one another's music, sports and news. A reggae song from Jamaica trends on TikTok in Trinidad the same week; a Bajan track is on Spotify in Barbados and Belize at once. This shared media diet builds the "one Caribbean people" identity that integration requires. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: e-commerce platforms allowing regional small businesses to sell across borders; digital payment systems; e-learning supporting CXC/CAPE students region-wide; co-ordinated emergency communication during hurricanes.
    Bullet 3: Suggest THREE measures that Caribbean governments may take to reduce the impact of the global media on the culture of the Caribbean. (6 marks)
    Bullet 4: Explain how EACH measure is likely to be successful. (6 marks)

    The "global media" problem: a flood of US/UK/Korean content (Netflix, MTV, K-pop, Hollywood) competing for Caribbean youth attention, eroding local language, foods, dress and identity.

    MeasureWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Set local-content broadcast quotas — require all radio and TV stations to play a minimum percentage (e.g., 40%) of Caribbean music and locally-produced programming. Quotas guarantee airtime for local artistes, who otherwise lose out to slick foreign products. Listeners gain regular exposure to home-grown content, raising its profile and commercial value, and providing a livelihood for the local creative sector.
    2. Subsidise the local creative industries — grants for filmmakers, music producers, animators; tax credits for shooting on location; build film & recording studios. Caribbean producers cannot match Hollywood budgets. State support narrows the production-quality gap, so local content is competitive enough that audiences willingly choose it. Subsidies also create jobs and grow exports (e.g., Jamaican animation studios).
    3. Embed Caribbean cultural studies in school curricula — folk music, dialect and history taught from primary level; school media-literacy lessons that teach students to critique foreign content. Catching children early roots them in their own culture before global media has a chance to define their tastes. Media-literate young people become discerning consumers rather than passive imitators of foreign trends.

    Other valid measures: support CARIFESTA and other regional cultural festivals; preserve and digitise indigenous languages and music archives; partner with the diaspora to promote Caribbean culture abroad; protect intellectual property of Caribbean artists.

    Question 6 — Section B Option: Consumer Affairs

    "Consumer Awareness: Practising Thrift" — written as a school-handbook article. (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Define the term 'thrift'. (2 marks)

    Thrift is the careful, wise and economical management of money and resources — spending only what is necessary, avoiding waste, and consistently saving a portion of one's income for future needs.

    1 mark for "wise/careful use of money"; 1 mark for the saving / avoidance-of-waste idea.

    Bullet 2: Explain ONE benefit, to students, of practising thrift. (2 marks)

    It builds financial security and independence. A student who saves a portion of every allowance, tutoring payment or holiday-job wage builds an emergency fund. When unexpected costs arise — a broken phone, school trip, university registration deposit — the thrifty student does not have to borrow from parents or a moneylender. Over time the habit compounds: by sixth-form they can fund their own laptop or contribute to tertiary fees, easing the burden on the family and developing self-reliance for adult life.

    1 mark for naming the benefit; 1 mark for explaining/expanding it.

    Bullet 3: Outline TWO ways in which globalization has increased students' demand for goods and services. (4 marks)
    1. Global advertising and social-media influencer culture. Through Instagram, TikTok and YouTube, Caribbean students see foreign celebrities and influencers wearing the latest sneakers, cosmetics and gadgets. Algorithms push these products relentlessly into students' feeds, making them feel they must own the same items to fit in. Demand for branded goods (Nike, iPhone, K-beauty cosmetics) has soared. (2 marks)
    2. E-commerce and faster international shipping. Globalisation has made foreign goods one click away — Amazon, Shein, AliExpress, eBay all ship to the Caribbean within days. Students with a debit card can order directly, often at prices below local stores. The choice is wider, the friction is lower, and demand has increased correspondingly. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: exposure to global music/film triggers demand for related merchandise; international fast-food chains expanding locally; greater study-abroad opportunities raising appetite for foreign experiences.
    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE strategies that students may employ in their efforts to practise thrift. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain why EACH strategy is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    StrategyWhy it works
    1. Make a written budget — write down every dollar received and every dollar spent, separate "needs" (lunch, transport) from "wants" (snacks, phone credit), set a fixed savings target (e.g., 20%). What gets measured gets managed. Most overspending is unconscious — small daily wastages add up. Seeing the totals at week-end forces students to face their habits and immediately reveals the easiest cuts.
    2. Open a savings account at a bank, credit union or school savings club with automatic deposits or "pay yourself first" before any spending. Money sitting in the wallet gets spent. Money in a savings account earns interest, requires effort to withdraw, and grows visibly. Credit-union savings clubs also pay dividends and teach the habit through peer accountability.
    3. Pack lunches and avoid impulse buying — bring food from home instead of buying at the canteen, wait 24 hours before any purchase over a set threshold, use shopping lists. Prepared meals cost a fraction of canteen prices and save thousands of dollars per term. The 24-hour rule kills most impulse buys — by the next day the urgency is gone and the student usually decides they don't really need the item.

    Other valid strategies: sell or swap old items rather than throw them away; use student discounts; learn DIY skills (haircuts, basic repairs); avoid peer pressure to overspend; earn extra through tutoring, baking or freelance gigs.

    Question 7 — Section B Option: Tourism

    "The Tourist Industry in the Caribbean — Benefits and Challenges" — speech for a Ministry of Tourism competition. (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Describe TWO ways in which Caribbean countries benefit from the tourist industry. (4 marks)
    1. Foreign exchange and government revenue. Tourists arrive with US dollars, Canadian dollars, Pounds and Euros — the hard currencies Caribbean economies need to pay for imports of fuel, food and machinery. Hotel taxes, departure taxes and VAT on tourist services also fund schools, roads and hospitals, allowing relatively small countries to maintain modern infrastructure. (2 marks)
    2. Employment creation. Tourism is one of the most labour-intensive industries — jobs run from chefs and housekeepers to dive instructors, taxi drivers, cultural performers, security and tour-guides. In countries like Antigua, Bahamas and Barbados, tourism directly or indirectly accounts for over a third of all jobs. Wages and tips circulate through the local economy via the multiplier effect. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: stimulus to other sectors (agriculture, fisheries, craft); diversification of single-crop economies; investment in transport infrastructure; preservation of cultural and natural heritage as marketable products.
    Bullet 2: Define the term 'economic leakage'. (2 marks)

    Economic leakage refers to the proportion of tourist spending that does NOT remain in the host country — for example, money paid to foreign-owned hotels and airlines, profits repatriated by multinational chains, payment for imported food and beverages served at all-inclusive resorts, and salaries paid to expatriate workers.

    1 mark for "money lost from the host economy"; 1 mark for naming a cause (foreign ownership, imports, repatriated profits, expatriate wages).

    Bullet 3: Explain ONE conflict/problem that may develop in the region between the tourist industry and agriculture. (3 marks)

    Competition for land, labour and water. Tourism is more profitable per acre than traditional agriculture, so coastal farms have been bought up and converted into hotels, golf courses, condos and marinas. Productive cane lands and small-farm holdings disappear, reducing the country's food production and forcing greater food imports (1 mark).

    The tourism sector also poaches agricultural labour — young workers prefer the higher wages, tips and air-conditioned environment of a hotel job to bending in the sun on a banana farm — leaving farms short-staffed (1 mark).

    Water and waste conflict — large resorts consume enormous quantities of water (laundry, swimming pools, irrigated lawns) and dump effluent that can pollute the streams and aquifers farmers depend on. The result is a steady erosion of the agricultural base that should be supplying the hotels — and increased dependence on expensive imports (1 mark).

    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE actions that the tourist industry could take to reduce the threats to the marine and coastal environments. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain why EACH action is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    Action by industryWhy it works
    1. Install proper sewage and waste-water treatment plants at every hotel and dive operation; ban the discharge of grey water and chemicals into the sea; treat all garbage on land. Untreated sewage smothers coral reefs and seagrass with algae and pathogens — destroying the very attraction tourists come to see. Modern treatment plants protect reefs, beaches and fisheries, ensuring the destination remains attractive long term.
    2. Limit watersport activities and enforce mooring buoys — designated swimming/diving zones, no-anchor reserves with permanent buoys, mandatory boater certification, ban on jet-skis near reefs. Anchors crush coral; jet-skis injure swimmers and dolphins; over-loaded reefs lose biodiversity. Zoning and buoys spread impact and protect critical habitat. Certification ensures that only trained operators take tourists into sensitive areas.
    3. Promote eco-tourism and visitor education — reef-friendly sunscreen rules, "leave-no-trace" hikes, educational signage, sponsor mangrove and coral-restoration projects, employ marine biologists. Visitors who understand the fragility of the environment behave better — they stop walking on reefs, refuse plastic straws and donate to conservation. Restoration projects (replanted mangroves, coral nurseries) physically rebuild what has been lost, and become tourist attractions themselves.

    Other valid actions: reduce single-use plastics; enforce strict environmental impact assessments before new development; cap cruise-ship arrivals to safe levels; partner with NGOs (e.g., CCMI, the Reef Foundation) for monitoring.
