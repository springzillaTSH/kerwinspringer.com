    Question 1 — Individual, Family and Society

    Extended family; ethnic groups + reasons for cultural diversity in the Caribbean; actions to become better parents. (20 marks)

    (a) Define the term 'extended family'. (2 marks)

    An extended family is a family unit in which the nuclear family (parents and children) lives together, or in close daily contact, with other relatives — such as grandparents, uncles, aunts and cousins — who all share resources and responsibilities for the raising of the children.

    1 mark for going beyond the nuclear unit (relatives); 1 mark for "live together / share responsibilities."

    (b)(i) State TWO ethnic groups found in the Caribbean. (2 marks)
    1. Africans / Afro-Caribbeans — descendants of enslaved West Africans brought during the Atlantic slave trade.
    2. East Indians / Indo-Caribbeans — descendants of indentured labourers brought from India after Emancipation (1838) to work the sugar estates, especially in Trinidad and Guyana.
    3. Other accepted: Europeans (British, French, Spanish, Dutch); Chinese; Syrian-Lebanese; Indigenous peoples (Kalinago, Taino, Garifuna); Mixed/Creole; Portuguese; Javanese (Suriname).

    1 mark each.

    (b)(ii) Outline TWO reasons for cultural diversity in the Caribbean region. (4 marks)
    1. The history of colonisation by different European powers. The British, French, Spanish and Dutch each colonised different islands, leaving behind their language, religion (Catholicism in St Lucia, Anglican in Barbados, Dutch Reformed in Suriname), legal system and architecture. (2 marks)
    2. Forced and voluntary migration of peoples. The slave trade brought West Africans; Indian and Chinese indentureship brought Asians; later waves of Syrian, Lebanese and Portuguese traders arrived as merchants. Each group brought its own foods, religions, music and family structures, all of which mixed locally to create a richly diverse population. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: indigenous Amerindian survival contributing language and farming practices; geographic proximity allowing inter-island migration; modern globalisation and tourism continuing the mixing.
    (c)(i) Suggest THREE actions that persons may take to help them become better parents. (6 marks)
    (c)(ii) Explain why EACH action suggested in (c)(i) is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    ActionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Attend parenting workshops / classes run by churches, the Ministry of Education, the Child Protection & Family Services Agency or NGOs. Workshops give parents proven techniques in child psychology, age-appropriate discipline, and communication. Practising these skills with peers and a facilitator means parents come home with concrete tools rather than the trial-and-error approach passed down from previous generations.
    2. Spend quality time with children — eat meals together, attend their school events, schedule one-on-one talks, do family activities every weekend. Quality time builds trust and emotional security. Children who feel heard are more likely to confide in their parents about peer pressure, bullying or difficulties at school — preventing small problems from becoming serious ones.
    3. Read widely and consult professionals — paediatricians, school guidance counsellors, books and parenting websites — and use positive (non-violent) discipline. Updated information helps parents avoid outdated practices (e.g., harsh corporal punishment) that research has linked to anxiety and aggression. Counsellors give tailored advice for issues like ADHD, learning differences or grief.

    Other valid actions: model the behaviour you expect; set clear rules and consistent consequences; build a support network with other parents; care for your own physical and mental health; balance work and family life.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 2 — Individual, Family and Society (Government & Institutions)

    Examples of institutions; voter-turnout table analysis; actions to improve group cohesion. (20 marks)

    (a) State ONE example of EACH of the following types of institutions. (2 marks)
    • Economic institution: a commercial bank (e.g., NCB, Republic Bank, RBC), the Stock Exchange, or a credit union.
    • Political institution: Parliament, the Senate / House of Representatives, a political party (e.g., PNP, JLP, PNM), the Cabinet.

    1 mark for each correct example.

    (b) Table 1 shows voter turnout (%) in Country X: 2007 = 60.4, 2011 = 53.2, 2016 = 46.4, 2021 = 37.8.
    (b)(i) Write TWO statements that describe the trend shown in the table. (2 marks)
    1. Voter turnout has fallen continuously from one election to the next — every reading is lower than the previous one (60.4% → 53.2% → 46.4% → 37.8%).
    2. The total decline over the 14-year period is large — turnout dropped by 22.6 percentage points between 2007 and 2021, more than a third of the original turnout. By 2021 fewer than 2 in 5 eligible voters cast a ballot.

    1 mark per accurate, data-supported statement.

    (b)(ii) Outline TWO factors that might have been responsible for the voter turnout in 2021. (4 marks)
    1. The COVID-19 pandemic. Many elections held in 2021 were affected by the pandemic — fear of crowds at polling stations, restrictions on campaigning, sickness on election day, and movement controls all reduced turnout. (2 marks)
    2. Apathy and loss of faith in politicians. A long pattern of broken promises, corruption scandals and a feeling that "all parties are the same" causes citizens — especially the young — to disengage. With no compelling candidate or burning issue, they simply stay home. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: rise of social-media disinformation and "vote-doesn't-matter" messaging; rainy/bad weather on election day; uncompetitive election (one party expected to win comfortably); cumbersome voter-ID process.
    (c)(i) Suggest THREE actions an organization may take to improve group cohesion among its members. (6 marks)
    (c)(ii) Explain how EACH action suggested in (c)(i) is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    ActionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Hold regular team-building activities — retreats, sports days, family fun days, off-site workshops. People bond outside the formal work setting. Shared experiences (winning a tug-of-war, cooking together) create informal trust that carries back into the workplace, where teams that like each other cooperate faster and resolve conflict more easily.
    2. Establish clear, shared goals and a strong vision/mission statement — communicated regularly through staff meetings, posters, the website. When every member understands what the organisation is trying to achieve, individual efforts pull in the same direction. Cohesion comes from purpose: people are bound together not by personalities but by a goal larger than themselves.
    3. Use open, two-way communication channels — open-door management, suggestion boxes, intranet forums, regular feedback surveys. Members who feel heard are far less likely to develop grievances that turn into factions. Transparency about decisions also kills the rumour-mill, which is a major driver of distrust and fragmentation in any organisation.

    Other valid actions: equitable rewards / recognition programmes; conflict-resolution training; democratic election of leaders; a strong induction programme for new members; uniforms and shared rituals that build identity.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 3 — Sustainable Development and Use of Resources

    Life expectancy; declining death rates; rate of natural increase & dependency ratio; actions to reduce brain drain. (20 marks)

    (a)(i) Define the term 'life expectancy'. (2 marks)

    Life expectancy is the average number of years that a person born in a particular country, in a given year, can expect to live based on current age-specific death rates.

    1 mark for "average number of years a person can expect to live"; 1 mark for the population/year context.

    (a)(ii) Outline ONE reason for declining death rates in the Caribbean. (2 marks)

    Major improvements in healthcare and public health. Vaccination programmes have eliminated many diseases (polio, measles); free or low-cost primary healthcare clinics, antibiotics, antiretrovirals, and modern hospitals catch and treat illness early. Combined with cleaner piped water, better sanitation and food safety, these advances mean far fewer Caribbean people die from preventable infections — lowering the overall death rate.

    1 mark for naming the reason; 1 mark for outlining how it works.

    (b)(i) State the TWO factors that are needed to determine the rate of natural increase of a population. (2 marks)
    1. The birth rate (number of live births per 1 000 people per year).
    2. The death rate (number of deaths per 1 000 people per year).

    Rate of natural increase = Birth rate − Death rate (often expressed as a percentage). 1 mark each.

    (b)(ii) Explain why it is important for a government to know the dependency ratio of the country. (2 marks)

    The dependency ratio compares the non-working population (under 15 + over 64) to the working-age population (15–64). Knowing it tells government how heavy a burden the workers are carrying — how many schools, pensions, hospitals and welfare programmes the working population must finance through taxes.

    It guides budget planning: a high ratio of children means more spending on schools and maternity care; a high ratio of elderly means more spending on pensions, geriatric care and long-term healthcare. It also signals long-term economic health — too few workers means slow growth and rising tax pressure.

    1 mark for explaining what the ratio measures; 1 mark for the planning/budget consequence.

    (c) Brain drain limits economic development in Caribbean countries. (i) Suggest THREE actions that governments may take to reduce brain drain. (6 marks) (ii) Explain why EACH is likely to be successful. (6 marks)

    "Brain drain" = the loss of highly-skilled nationals (doctors, nurses, teachers, engineers, IT professionals) who emigrate, usually to North America or the UK, in search of better salaries and conditions.

    Government actionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Improve salaries and working conditions for skilled professionals — competitive pay scales for doctors, nurses, teachers; safer hospitals and schools; modern equipment. The single biggest driver of emigration is the income gap. A Jamaican nurse can earn 4–6× more in the US. Closing that gap (or even narrowing it) — together with manageable workloads — removes the financial reason to leave and rewards staying.
    2. Bond government scholarship recipients — students who receive state-funded tertiary education must work in the country for an agreed number of years (e.g., 3–5). Bonding ensures the country gets a return on its investment in training. Even when bonded professionals later emigrate, the country has already received several years of productive service and the trainees' presence eases shortages while another batch is being trained.
    3. Establish diaspora-engagement programmes — encourage skilled emigrants to return through tax breaks, dual citizenship, "skills-for-development" exchanges and remote-work positions. Many diaspora professionals want to give back. Formal programmes (e.g., UWI's diaspora portal, Jamaica's Returning Residents Programme) make it easier for them to teach short courses, invest in start-ups, or move home permanently — essentially reversing the brain-drain into a "brain-gain".

    Other valid actions: invest in research facilities at UWI / UTech / UG so academics don't need to emigrate; partner with the private sector to create high-skill jobs; subsidise housing and student-loan repayment for staff who stay; tighten work-permit reciprocity with destination countries.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 4 — Regional Integration (Essay)

    "Caribbean Regional Integration — Threats and Solutions" — five-bullet structured essay. (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Define the term 'globalization'. (2 marks)

    Globalization is the process by which countries, economies and cultures around the world are becoming increasingly interconnected and interdependent — through faster international trade, the movement of people, the spread of communications technology, and the operations of multinational corporations.

    1 mark for the interconnected/interdependent idea; 1 mark for an enabling mechanism (trade, MNCs, ICT, migration).

    Bullet 2: Explain how any TWO of the following can hinder Caribbean regional development: insularity; multinational corporations; differences in stages of growth and development. (6 marks — 3 marks each)

    Two of the strongest answers below — pick whichever two you know best.

    1. Insularity — the "small-island" mentality where each Caribbean state acts as if its own interests come above the region's. Island governments may refuse to ratify CSME provisions, drag their feet on free movement, or hoard tariff revenue. Insular politicians appeal to nationalism for short-term votes ("foreigners taking our jobs") which blocks the integration agenda. The result is duplicated agencies, fragmented markets too small for serious investors, and a Caribbean that loses bargaining power in world trade. (3 marks)
    2. Multinational corporations (MNCs) — large foreign-owned firms (Sandals, Coca-Cola, big banks, oil majors) operate across the Caribbean but repatriate most of their profits. They squeeze out small Caribbean producers, dictate terms to governments through lobbying, and create branch-plant economies dependent on head-office decisions made in Miami or London. They also push standardised "global" products that erode local culture. Their dominance limits the share of growth that Caribbean people themselves enjoy and undermines the integration of regional industries. (3 marks)
    3. Differences in stages of growth and development — Trinidad & Tobago and Bahamas (high-income, oil/tourism) sit alongside Haiti and parts of Belize (low-income, fragile). The richer states fear being flooded with migrant labour or being forced to subsidise weaker ones; the poorer states fear being locked out of competitive markets. These differences make a "one-size-fits-all" CSME hard to negotiate and create resentment that slows integration. (3 marks)
    Bullet 3: Outline ONE way in which the CSME helps to solve the problems of regional development. (2 marks)

    Free movement of skilled labour and the right of establishment. The CSME allows graduates, sportspeople, artistes, media workers, nurses, teachers and other CARICOM Skilled Nationals to live and work in any member state without a work permit, and lets entrepreneurs establish a business in any member state on the same terms as locals. This addresses regional unemployment in poorer states (workers can move to where the jobs are), fills skill shortages in richer states, and encourages cross-border investment that none of the small markets could attract individually.

    1 mark for naming the CSME mechanism; 1 mark for outlining the development benefit.

    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE actions that citizens may take to support regional integration. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain ONE reason why EACH action is likely to be successful. (3 marks)
    Citizen actionWhy it works
    1. Buy local and CARICOM-made products instead of extra-regional imports — choose Trinidadian curry, Guyanese rice, Jamaican Blue Mountain coffee, Bajan rum. Every dollar spent on a CARICOM product supports a regional producer, reducing leakage of foreign exchange and strengthening intra-regional trade — the lifeblood of integration.
    2. Travel and study within the region — visit other CARICOM states for vacation; choose UWI campuses or other regional universities; participate in CARIFESTA, CARIFTA, sporting tournaments. Personal contact builds the shared "Caribbean identity" that the politicians have struggled to create. Citizens who have lived in another island become lifelong advocates for integration and form business and friendship networks across borders.
    3. Use the media and social media to share Caribbean news, music, art and success stories instead of only consuming North-American or British content. The diet of a culture follows the diet of its information. Watching CMC news, sharing Caribbean YouTubers and streaming regional music keeps the region's stories alive in citizens' minds, builds pride, and counterbalances the cultural pull of the global North.

    Other valid actions: support the West Indies cricket team and other regional sporting franchises; attend or volunteer for community/Caribbean-themed events; learn about CARICOM's institutions; advocate to politicians for full CSME implementation.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 5 — Section B Option: Communication

    "Protecting the Work of Artistes — Role of the Media". (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Define the term 'copyright'. (2 marks)

    Copyright is a legal right that gives the creator of an original artistic, literary, musical or dramatic work the EXCLUSIVE right to reproduce, distribute, perform, broadcast or adapt that work for a set period of time (typically the life of the author plus 50 or 70 years).

    1 mark for "exclusive right of the creator"; 1 mark for the protected uses (copying, performing, distributing, etc.).

    Bullet 2: State TWO types of creative work that are copyright protected. (2 marks)
    1. Musical compositions and sound recordings — songs, soca, dancehall riddims, original lyrics.
    2. Literary works — novels, poetry, short stories, scripts, news articles.
    3. Other accepted: artistic works (paintings, photographs, sculptures); films & videos; choreography; computer software; broadcasts.

    1 mark each.

    Bullet 3: Outline TWO ways in which artistes can protect their work. (4 marks)
    1. Register the work with the relevant Copyright Office or Collective Management Organisation (e.g., JACAP in Jamaica, COTT in Trinidad & Tobago, COSCAP in Barbados). Registration creates a dated, official record of authorship that is invaluable in court if the work is later copied; it also entitles the artiste to royalties whenever their music is broadcast on radio/TV. (2 marks)
    2. Use written contracts and watermarks / digital tags on every release. A clear contract with a producer, manager or record label spells out who owns the master and what royalty share the artiste keeps. Digital fingerprints on streaming platforms (e.g., Content ID) automatically detect unauthorised re-uploads of the song. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: publish under a recognisable trademark/stage name; deposit copies with the National Library; place a © notice on every recording, video and merchandise item; pursue infringers through the courts.
    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE actions artistes may take to discourage the mass media from publishing information that may be harmful to the local community. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain why EACH action is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    ActionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Lead public-awareness campaigns — record positive songs, jingles and music videos against gun violence, gender-based violence, drug abuse; speak at community events. Artistes are role models, especially for young people. A campaign fronted by a popular dancehall, soca or reggae act reaches the same audience that consumes harmful content — but turns the influence in a positive direction. Listeners who admire the artiste are more likely to internalise the message.
    2. Refuse to record, perform or feature on songs that glorify violence, drugs or misogyny, and publicly explain the refusal. Media outlets follow the supply. If big artistes consistently decline harmful content, producers and DJs have nothing inflammatory to play. The public refusal also pressures peers to do the same — it shifts industry norms.
    3. Lobby government and the Broadcasting Commission for stricter content standards and partner with media houses to draft a voluntary "code of ethics". Regulatory pressure carries legal weight — the Broadcasting Commission can fine or suspend stations that breach the rules. Artistes' collective voice gives politicians political cover to act. Voluntary codes covered by industry buy-in reduce harmful content without infringing freedom of expression.

    Other valid actions: use social-media platforms for direct positive messaging; partner with schools/PTAs for outreach; sue for defamation when false personal stories are published; form an artistes' union to negotiate with media.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 6 — Section B Option: Consumer Affairs

    "Consumer Livelihood and the Rising Cost of Living". (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: State TWO types of consumers found in the Caribbean. (2 marks)
    1. Domestic / household consumers — ordinary individuals and families buying food, clothing, electricity, transport for personal use.
    2. Industrial / commercial consumers — businesses and government agencies purchasing raw materials, machinery, office supplies and services to use in their own operations.
    3. Other accepted: wholesale consumers (resellers); foreign/visitor consumers (tourists); institutional consumers (schools, hospitals).

    1 mark each.

    Bullet 2: Describe ONE factor that might influence a consumer's demand for goods or services. (2 marks)

    Income level. The amount of disposable income a consumer has after taxes is the single biggest determinant of how much they can buy. As wages, remittances or business profits rise, consumers move from buying only basic necessities (rice, flour, public transport) to purchasing more comfort goods (cars, restaurant meals, vacations). When income falls — for example during a recession or after a natural disaster — demand for non-essentials drops sharply while demand for inferior goods (cheaper alternatives) may rise.

    1 mark for naming the factor; 1 mark for an accurate description of how it affects demand.

    Bullet 3: Outline TWO benefits of credit unions to consumers in the Caribbean region. (4 marks)
    1. Lower interest rates on loans and higher rates on savings. Because credit unions are member-owned cooperatives (not profit-driven banks), surplus earnings are returned to members as dividends and used to subsidise lower lending rates. A working-class Caribbean family can therefore borrow for a refrigerator or school fees at a far cheaper rate than the high-street bank would offer. (2 marks)
    2. Financial inclusion for low-income members. Credit unions accept members with small deposits and informal incomes (taxi drivers, market vendors, farmers) who often cannot meet the strict collateral requirements of commercial banks. They provide financial education, easy savings products, and a sense of community ownership. (2 marks)
    3. Other accepted: shared-back-end services (insurance, mortgages); democratic "one member, one vote"; profits stay in the local community; member education programmes.
    Bullet 4: Suggest THREE actions that individuals may take to minimize the effects of the rising cost of living. (6 marks)
    Bullet 5: Explain why EACH action is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    ActionWhy it is likely to succeed
    1. Prepare and stick to a household budget — track every dollar, separate "needs" from "wants", set savings targets. Rising prices punish unconscious spending. A written budget forces the consumer to see where their money actually goes and to cut waste (subscriptions, takeout, impulse buys). Even a 10% trim across discretionary spending offsets a similar rise in the cost of essentials.
    2. Shop smart — buy in bulk, compare prices across supermarkets, use sales and coupons, choose store-brand over name-brand staples, prefer locally-grown produce. Local produce is cheaper because it avoids import duty and shipping. Bulk and store-brand purchases drop unit prices significantly. These small everyday savings compound across hundreds of grocery items per month.
    3. Grow a kitchen garden and develop a side-hustle — vegetables in pots, raise a few chickens; offer tutoring, baking, hairdressing, or freelance gigs online. Backyard food directly substitutes for items in the shopping basket; a side-hustle adds income that grows with prices. Together they reduce dependence on a fixed wage that may not be keeping up with inflation.

    Other valid actions: switch to LED bulbs / solar to cut utility bills; carpool to save fuel; refinance high-interest debt; use credit unions; reduce dining out; "buy used" for clothes and electronics.

    Total: 20 marks

    Question 7 — Section B Option: Tourism

    "Development in the Tourism Industry". (20 marks)

    Bullet 1: Define the term 'tourism'. (2 marks)

    Tourism is the activity of people travelling to, and staying in, places outside their usual environment for a continuous period of less than one year, for leisure, business, health, religious, educational or other personal purposes — and the industries that supply their accommodation, transport, food and entertainment needs.

    Bullet 2: Outline the impact of EACH of the following on the development of the tourism industry in the Caribbean: infrastructure; disposable income; climate. (6 marks — 2 marks each)
    1. Infrastructure — modern international airports (e.g., Sangster International, Piarco), upgraded cruise piers, paved highways, reliable water and electricity, and high-speed internet are essential. Without these, large airlines, cruise lines and international hotel brands will not invest. Caribbean countries that have invested in infrastructure (Bahamas, Barbados, Jamaica) attract far more visitors than those that have not. (2 marks)
    2. Disposable income (in source markets) — Caribbean tourism depends on the spare cash of consumers in North America and Europe. When the US/UK economies are strong and households have disposable income, tourist arrivals rise. During recessions (2008, 2020 COVID) bookings collapse because vacations are the first non-essential to be cut. The Caribbean is therefore very vulnerable to events outside its control. (2 marks)
    3. Climate — warm temperatures (25–30°C) year-round, sunshine, white-sand beaches and clear seas are the Caribbean's primary attraction. The "winter tourist season" (December–April) coincides with cold weather in source markets — that's when most of the income is earned. However, hurricanes (June–November), rising sea levels and bleaching coral reefs threaten the very climate that the industry depends on. (2 marks)
    Bullet 3: Suggest THREE ways in which communications technology may be used to improve tourism in CARICOM. (6 marks)
    Bullet 4: Explain why EACH way is likely to be successful. (6 marks)
    Use of communications technologyWhy it works
    1. Social media marketing & influencer partnerships — Instagram, TikTok, YouTube creators showcasing CARICOM destinations to global audiences. Social media reaches millions of potential visitors at a fraction of the cost of TV ads. Authentic user-generated content (drone videos of beaches, food vlogs) is more persuasive than a glossy brochure, and influencers' followers convert well into bookings. The Caribbean Tourism Organization can target campaigns by season and source market.
    2. Online booking platforms and dynamic-pricing systems — direct hotel websites, Airbnb, Expedia integrations, virtual tours. Visitors can compare rates, see real photos, and book in minutes from anywhere in the world — making CARICOM as easy to choose as any other global destination. Dynamic pricing fills empty rooms in low season at discounted rates and maximises revenue in high season.
    3. Mobile apps for visitor experience — multilingual destination apps with maps, attraction tickets, ride-sharing, currency converters, emergency contacts, e-customs/immigration. Smooth, technology-supported experiences increase visitor satisfaction and the likelihood of repeat visits and positive reviews. Faster e-immigration also reduces airport queues, which travellers consistently rank as a major pain point.

    Other valid uses: AI chatbots for hotel customer service in multiple languages; data analytics to predict demand and target promotions; AR/VR previews of destinations; digital "Caribbean passport" for hassle-free intra-regional travel; cybersecurity tools to protect visitor data.
